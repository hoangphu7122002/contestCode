    Factorial modulo   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ ¶

    In some cases it is necessary to consider complex formulas modulo some prime   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ , containing factorials in both numerator and denominator, like such that you encounter in the formula for Binomial coefficients. We consider the case when   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$  is relatively small. This problem makes only sense when the factorials appear in both numerator and denominator of fractions. Otherwise   p!<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi><mo>!</mo></math>$p!$  and subsequent terms will reduce to zero. But in fractions the factors of   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$  can cancel, and the resulting expression will be non-zero modulo   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ .

    Thus, formally the task is: You want to calculate   n!modp<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>n</mi><mo>!</mo><mo lspace="thickmathspace" rspace="thickmathspace">mod</mo><mi>p</mi></math>$n! \bmod p$ , without taking all the multiple factors of   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$  into account that appear in the factorial. Imaging you write down the prime factorization of   n!<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>n</mi><mo>!</mo></math>$n!$ , remove all factors   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ , and compute the product modulo   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ . We will denote this modified factorial with   n!%p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>n</mi><msub><mo>!</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">%</mi><mi>p</mi></mrow></msub></math>$n!_{\%p}$ . For instance   7!%p≡1⋅2⋅1⏟3⋅4⋅52⏟6⋅7≡2mod3<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mn>7</mn><msub><mo>!</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">%</mi><mi>p</mi></mrow></msub><mo>≡</mo><mn>1</mn><mo>⋅</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>⋅</mo><munder><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mn>1</mn><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mn>3</mn></mrow></munder><mo>⋅</mo><mn>4</mn><mo>⋅</mo><mn>5</mn><munder><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mn>2</mn><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mn>6</mn></mrow></munder><mo>⋅</mo><mn>7</mn><mo>≡</mo><mn>2</mn><mo lspace="thickmathspace" rspace="thickmathspace">mod</mo><mn>3</mn></math>$7!_{\%p} \equiv 1 \cdot 2 \cdot \underbrace{1}_{3} \cdot 4 \cdot 5 \underbrace{2}_{6} \cdot 7 \equiv 2 \bmod 3$ .

    Learning how to effectively calculate this modified factorial allows us to quickly calculate the value of the various combinatorial formulas (for example, Binomial coefficients).

    The value of the last partial block can be calculated separately in   O(p)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(p)$ .

    This leaves only the last element of each block. If we hide the already handled elements, we can see the following pattern:

      n!%p=…⋅1⏟⋅…⋅2⏟⋅…⋅…⋅(p−1)⏟⋅…⋅1⏟⋅…⋅1⏟⋅…⋅2⏟⋯<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><mi>n</mi><msub><mo>!</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">%</mi><mi>p</mi></mrow></msub><mo>=</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mrow><mo>…</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mo>⋅</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mrow><mo>…</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mo>⋅</mo><mo>…</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mrow><mo>…</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></mrow><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mo>⋅</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mrow><mo>…</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mo>⋅</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mrow><mo>…</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mo>⋅</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="OP"><munder><mrow><mo>…</mo><mo>⋅</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow><mo>⏟</mo></munder></mrow><mo>⋯</mo></math>$$n!_{\%p} = \underbrace{ \ldots \cdot 1 } \cdot \underbrace{ \ldots \cdot 2} \cdot \ldots \cdot \underbrace{ \ldots \cdot (p-1)} \cdot \underbrace{ \ldots \cdot 1 } \cdot \underbrace{ \ldots \cdot 1} \cdot \underbrace{ \ldots \cdot 2} \cdots$$ 

    This again is a modified factorial, only with a much smaller dimension. It's   ⌊n/p⌋!%p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌊</mo><mi>n</mi><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mo>/</mo></mrow><mi>p</mi><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌋</mo><msub><mo>!</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">%</mi><mi>p</mi></mrow></msub></math>$\lfloor n / p \rfloor !_{\%p}$ .

    Thus, during the calculation of the modified factorial   n%p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>n</mi><msub><mstyle scriptlevel="0"><mspace width="-0.167em"></mspace></mstyle><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">%</mi><mi>p</mi></mrow></msub></math>$n\!_{\%p}$  we did   O(p)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(p)$  operations and are left with the calculation of   ⌊n/p⌋!%p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌊</mo><mi>n</mi><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mo>/</mo></mrow><mi>p</mi><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌋</mo><msub><mo>!</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">%</mi><mi>p</mi></mrow></msub></math>$\lfloor n / p \rfloor !_{\%p}$ . We have a recursive formula. The recursion depth is   O(logp⁡n)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>log</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo data-mjx-texclass="NONE">⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(\log_p n)$ , and therefore the complete asymptotic behavior of the algorithm is   O(plogp⁡n)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><msub><mi>log</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo data-mjx-texclass="NONE">⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(p \log_p n)$ .

    Notice, if you precompute the factorials   0!, 1!, 2!, …, (p−1)!<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mn>0</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mn>1</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mn>2</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mo>…</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>!</mo></math>$0!,~ 1!,~ 2!,~ \dots,~ (p-1)!$  modulo   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ , then the complexity will just be   O(logp⁡n)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>log</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo data-mjx-texclass="NONE">⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(\log_p n)$ .

    Implementation¶

    We don't need recursion because this is a case of tail recursion and thus can be easily implemented using iteration. In the following implementation we precompute the factorials   0!, 1!, …, (p−1)!<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mn>0</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mn>1</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mo>…</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>!</mo></math>$0!,~ 1!,~ \dots,~ (p-1)!$ , and thus have the runtime   O(p+logp⁡n)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><mo>+</mo><msub><mi>log</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo data-mjx-texclass="NONE">⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(p + \log_p n)$ . If you need to call the function multiple times, then you can do the precomputation outside of the function and do the computation of   n!%p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>n</mi><msub><mo>!</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">%</mi><mi>p</mi></mrow></msub></math>$n!_{\%p}$  in   O(logp⁡n)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>log</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo data-mjx-texclass="NONE">⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(\log_p n)$  time.

    int factmod(int n, int p) {
    vector<int> f(p);
    f[0] = 1;
    for (int i = 1; i < p; i++)
        f[i] = f[i-1] * i % p;

    int res = 1;
    while (n > 1) {
        if ((n/p) % 2)
            res = p - res;
        res = res * f[n%p] % p;
        n /= p;
    }
    return res;
}

    Alternative, if you only have limit memory and can't afford storing all factorials, you can also just remember the factorials that you need, sort them, and then compute them in one sweep by computing the factorials   0!, 1!, 2!, …, (p−1)!<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mn>0</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mn>1</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mn>2</mn><mo>!</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mo>…</mo><mo>,</mo><mtext>&nbsp;</mtext><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>p</mi><mo>−</mo><mn>1</mn><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>!</mo></math>$0!,~ 1!,~ 2!,~ \dots,~ (p-1)!$  in a loop without storing them explicitly.

    Multiplicity of   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ ¶

    If we want to compute a Binomial coefficient modulo   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ , then we additionally need the multiplicity of the   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$  in   n<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>n</mi></math>$n$ , i.e. the number of times   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$  occurs in the prime factorization of   n<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>n</mi></math>$n$ , or number of times we erased   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$  during the computation of the modified factorial.

    Legendre's formula gives us a way to compute this in   O(logp⁡n)<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>O</mi><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><msub><mi>log</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo data-mjx-texclass="NONE">⁡</mo><mi>n</mi><mo stretchy="false">)</mo></math>$O(\log_p n)$  time. The formula gives the multiplicity   νp<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><msub><mi>ν</mi><mi>p</mi></msub></math>$\nu_p$  as:

      νp(n!)=∑i=1∞⌊npi⌋<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block"><msub><mi>ν</mi><mi>p</mi></msub><mo stretchy="false">(</mo><mi>n</mi><mo>!</mo><mo stretchy="false">)</mo><mo>=</mo><munderover><mo data-mjx-texclass="OP">∑</mo><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi>i</mi><mo>=</mo><mn>1</mn></mrow><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow></munderover><mrow data-mjx-texclass="INNER"><mo data-mjx-texclass="OPEN">⌊</mo><mfrac><mi>n</mi><msup><mi>p</mi><mi>i</mi></msup></mfrac><mo data-mjx-texclass="CLOSE">⌋</mo></mrow></math>$$\nu_p(n!) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p^i} \right\rfloor$$ 

    Thus we get the implementation:

    int multiplicity_factorial(int n, int p) {
    int count = 0;
    do {
        n /= p;
        count += n;
    } while (n);
    return count;
}

    This formula can be proven very easily using the same ideas that we did in the previous sections. Remove all elements that don't contain the factor   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$ . This leaves   ⌊n/p⌋<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌊</mo><mi>n</mi><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mo>/</mo></mrow><mi>p</mi><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌋</mo></math>$\lfloor n/p \rfloor$  element remaining. If we remove the factor   p<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mi>p</mi></math>$p$  from each of them, we get the product   1⋅2⋯⌊n/p⌋=⌊n/p⌋!<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><mn>1</mn><mo>⋅</mo><mn>2</mn><mo>⋯</mo><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌊</mo><mi>n</mi><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mo>/</mo></mrow><mi>p</mi><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌋</mo><mo>=</mo><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌊</mo><mi>n</mi><mrow data-mjx-texclass="ORD"><mo>/</mo></mrow><mi>p</mi><mo fence="false" stretchy="false">⌋</mo><mo>!</mo></math>$1 \cdot 2 \cdots \lfloor n/p \rfloor = \lfloor n/p \rfloor !$ , and again we have a recursion.
